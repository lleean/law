## 💡 Zentrale Konzepte & Notizen
> [!ABSTRACT] Zusammenfassung
> 1. **Internet Law** is a relatively new and expanding legal domain that integrates various disciplines like jurisdiction, human rights, and copyright to address global digital challenges.
> 
> 2. **Technological advancement** is characterized by rapid growth laws (e.g., Moore's Law and the even faster growth of AI computing power), which have transformed media into a fluid, networkable, and easily manipulated form.
> 
> 3. **Regulation in cyberspace** operates through four modalities—law, social norms, markets, and architecture—where technical "code" often displaces traditional law through mechanisms like algorithmic enforcement.
> 

### 1. Technological Foundations & Digitization

- **Digitization Process:** Information is rendered into binary digits, supported by powerful hardware (microchips) and networks (optical fibers).

- **Laws of Advancement:** Progress is governed by Moore’s Law (microprocessor power), Metcalfe’s Law (network value), and the Fibre Law (capacity doubling every nine months).

- **AI Growth:** Since 2012, AI computing power has been doubling every 3.4 months, significantly exceeding Moore's Law.

- **Layered Model:** The internet is structured into three layers: the **physical layer** (hardware/networks), the **logical layer** (protocols/software), and the **content layer** (human communication).

### 2. Societal & Legal Implications

- **Media Characteristics:** Digital media is freed from tangible mediums, allows for perfect copies, and treats **data as an asset**.

- **Shift in Production:** The move from "user to creator" promises individual freedom and innovation but introduces downsides like **echo chambers**, fake news, and private power concentration.

• **The "Law of the Horse":** A central theoretical debate asks whether internet law is merely a collection of existing rules applied to a new field (the "law of the horse") or a phenomenon that "illuminates the entire law".

### 3. Modalities of Regulation (Lessig)

- **Four Modalities:** Regulation is achieved through Law, Social Norms, Markets, and **Architecture (Code)**.

- **Code as Law:** Architecture in cyberspace is not fixed; code can be modified to monitor or restrict access, effectively displacing traditional legal norms.

- **Examples of Technical Enforcement:** Digital Rights Management (DRM) and algorithmic enforcement are ways where technical architecture replaces standard legal contracts.
