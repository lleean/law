## 💡 Zentrale Konzepte & Notizen
> [!ABSTRACT] Zusammenfassung
> 1. **Internet Governance** has transitioned from a centralized US military-research project to a privatized **multi-stakeholder model** led by **ICANN**, which manages global domain names and technical protocols.
> 2. The regulatory debate features a clash between **cyber-libertarians**, who view cyberspace as independent of physical location, and **cyber-realists**, who argue that states can effectively regulate local effects of extraterritorial digital acts.
> 3. **Jurisdiction in cyberspace** is increasingly asserted by national courts through de-indexing and content removal orders, leading to significant international legal standoffs regarding **global censorship** and the **Right to be Forgotten**.

### 1. Internet Infrastructure and ICANN
- **Technical Evolution:** The internet's history is defined by **packet-switching** (breaking data into packets sent via different routes) and the development of **TCP/IP** as non-proprietary standards.

- **Salient Design:** Originally a "dumb" network, it prioritizes **end-to-end connectivity**, allowing innovation at the edges rather than the center, though this has shifted toward closed platforms over time.

- **ICANN's Role:** A non-profit corporation that coordinates the **Domain Name System (DNS)** root zone, manages IP addresses, and oversees the **UDRP** for resolving domain disputes.

- **Domain Names:** Divided into **gTLDs** (generic, like .com or .swiss) and **ccTLDs** (country-coded, like .ch or .uk).

### 2. Theoretical Modalities of Regulation
- **Lessig’s 4 Modalities:** Cyberspace is regulated by **Law**, **Social Norms**, **Markets**, and **Architecture (Code)**.

- **Code as Law:** Technical architecture is not fixed; code can be modified to restrict access or monitor behavior, often **displacing traditional law** (e.g., Digital Rights Management replacing copyright contracts).

- **Sovereignty Debate:**
	- **Johnson/Post:** Argue that the lack of physical borders makes local government control over online behavior illegitimate.

	- **Goldsmith:** Contends that territorial sovereignty supports the regulation of persons and hardware within a territory to control the local effects of global acts.


### 3. Jurisdiction and Global Content Removal
- **UDRP:** A mandatory administrative procedure for all registrars to resolve trademark-based domain disputes (e.g., **cybersquatting**) quickly and without required legal representation.

- **The "Lowest Common Denominator" Risk:** If websites must comply with the most restrictive jurisdiction's laws to avoid liability, the global internet risks being governed by the least free standards.

- **Global Injunctions:** Recent rulings in **Canada (Equustek)** and the **EU (Glawischnig-Piesczek)** have seen courts order platforms to remove content or de-index sites **worldwide**, though US courts have sometimes blocked the domestic enforcement of such foreign orders based on **Section 230 CDA** and First Amendment concerns.

## ⚖️ Relevante Normen & Rechtsprechung
**Gesetze:**
- **UDRP:** Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy.
- **GDPR (EU):** Enshrines the **"Right to be Forgotten"** (Art. 17).
- **Section 230 CDA (US):** Provides immunity for interactive computer service providers against being treated as the publisher of third-party content.
- **Additional Frameworks:** Digital Markets Act (DMA), Digital Services Act (DSA), US Cloud Act, and the 2024 US TikTok ban.

**Leitentscheide:**
- **Yahoo! Case** **(France, 2000):** One of the first major cases asserting jurisdiction over a US-based site for violating local laws (prohibiting Nazi items).
	- The Dispute: French NGOs sued Yahoo! Inc. because its US-based auction site allowed the sale of Nazi memorabilia, which is a criminal offense in France,.
	- Key Ruling: The French court asserted jurisdiction because the "harm" (viewing Nazi items) occurred on French territory,.
	- Implementation: The court did not order a worldwide ban; instead, it required Yahoo! to use geographic filtering to block users with French IP addresses from accessing the content,.
	- Significance: This was one of the first major cases to prove that a state could regulate the local effects of content hosted in a different country,.

- **Google Spain** **(CJEU, 2014):** Established the "Right to be Forgotten," allowing individuals to request the removal of specific search engine links.
	- The Dispute: Mario Costeja González requested that Google remove links to 15-year-old newspaper notices about his past debts, arguing they were no longer relevant,.
	- Key Ruling: Established the "Right to be Forgotten" (the right to de-referencing),. The court ruled that search engines are data controllers and must balance the individual’s privacy rights against the public's interest in the information,.
	- Jurisdiction: The court applied EU law to Google (a US company) because it had a Spanish subsidiary dedicated to selling advertising space,.
	- Significance: It forced search engines to evaluate "adequacy" and "relevance" of information, though a later 2019 ruling clarified that this right generally only applies within EU borders, not globally,.

- **Equustek v. Google** **(Canada, 2017):** Upheld a worldwide de-indexing injunction against Google to stop the sale of misappropriated trade secrets.
	- The Dispute: Equustek sued a distributor for stealing trade secrets,. When the distributor ignored court orders and continued selling products online, Equustek asked Google to de-index the infringer’s websites worldwide,.
	- Key Ruling: The Supreme Court of Canada upheld a global injunction,. They argued that a de-indexing order limited to "google.ca" would be ineffective because the harm was global and users could easily switch versions of the search engine,.
	- Significance: This case marked a turning point where a national court asserted that its orders must have worldwide effect to be meaningful in the borderless digital economy,.


- **Glawischnig-Piesczek v. Facebook** **(CJEU, 2019):** Ruled that EU member states can order host providers to remove identical or equivalent unlawful information worldwide.
	- The Dispute: An Austrian politician sued Facebook to remove a defamatory comment posted by an anonymous user,.
	- Key Ruling: The CJEU ruled that a platform can be ordered to remove not only the specific unlawful post but also identical and equivalent content (information conveying the same message with slightly different wording),.
	- Implementation: Crucially, the court held that EU law does not prevent Member States from ordering these removals worldwide, provided they act within the framework of international law,.
	- Significance: This decision is controversial because it essentially allows one country’s defamation standards to dictate what users globally can see, raising fears of "global censorship",.
